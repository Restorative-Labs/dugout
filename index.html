    3 · Run the checkpoints
    1

    Stance & setup

    ▾
    ● STANCE
    Weight split 50/50 — balanced and athletic
    • Feet a little wider than shoulders, weight on the balls of the feet
    • Knees bent, athletic — like a shortstop ready to move
    • Hands relaxed near the back shoulder, bat off the shoulder
    • Eyes level, both eyes on the pitcher
    Say it like a coach"Show me your athletic position — same one you'd use guarding somebody in basketball."

    On film: freeze the first frame. Draw a vertical line from his head down — it should split his body roughly in half. Circle his hands: are they quiet and near the back shoulder, or drifting?

    2

    Load

    ▾
    ● LOAD
    Small shift back — weight loads inside the back foot
    • Small, slow shift of weight into the back leg as the pitcher lifts his leg
    • Hands move back an inch or two — not a big wrap around the head
    • Front shoulder stays closed, pointed at the pitcher
    Say it like a coach"Load slow and early. If you're rushing your load, you're already late."

    On film: freeze at the top of the load. Draw a line down the back leg — his weight should be inside that back foot, not spilling over it. An arrow showing hands drifting forward too early is one of the most common fixes at this age.

    3

    Stride

    ▾
    ● STRIDE
    Foot goes forward — hands stay back
    • Short, soft step toward the pitcher — land on the inside of the front foot
    • Head stays still; it shouldn't bob down or lunge forward
    • Hands stay back while the foot goes forward ("separation")
    Say it like a coach"Step to the pitcher and land soft, like you're stepping on ice. Foot goes forward, hands stay back."

    On film: freeze at front-foot touchdown. Circle his head in the setup frame, then step frames forward — the circle shouldn't move much. Draw a line at his stride foot: is it aimed at the pitcher or stepping "in the bucket" (away from the plate)?

    4

    Fire the hips

    ▾
    ● HIPS FIRE
    Belt turns first — back heel up, front leg firm
    • Hips rotate first, then hands — the belt buckle turns to face the pitcher
    • Back foot releases and pivots up onto the toe — let it turn, don't grind it down
    • Front leg firms up straight, like a wall to rotate against
    Say it like a coach"Your hands don't make power — your hips do. Turn fast and let the bat come along for the ride."

    On film: step frame by frame from foot-down to contact. Use the angle tool on the front knee — it should firm up toward straight (roughly 150–180°) at contact. If the belt turns at the same time as the hands instead of before, that's your talking point.

    5

    Contact & extension

    ▾
    ● CONTACT
    Eyes on the ball — hit through it
    • Eyes down on the contact point — head stays on the ball
    • Bat is roughly level or slightly up through the zone, not chopping down or looping
    • Arms extend through the ball after contact, not stopping at it
    Say it like a coach"Hit through the ball, not at it. Pretend there are three baseballs in a row and you have to hit all three."

    On film: this is the money frame — step until the bat meets the ball. Draw a line along the bat barrel: slightly up toward the pitcher is ideal. Circle his eyes: are they on the contact point or already peeking where the ball is going?

    6

    Finish

    ▾
    ● FINISH
    Balanced over the line — hold the finish
    • Full follow-through, bat finishing high around the shoulder or back
    • Balanced — he could hold the pose for three seconds without wobbling
    • Chest facing the pitcher, back toe pointed down
    Say it like a coach"Hold your finish like a statue. If you fall over, the swing got too big somewhere."

    On film: freeze the last frame. Draw a vertical line through his center — is his weight balanced over it, or drifting toward the plate or the dugout? Balance at the finish usually tells you the truth about everything before it.
